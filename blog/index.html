  • Starsight v. Skyward

    Jan 18, 2020 - Reviews blog - Books

    Having gone through both Skyward and its sequel, Starsight, by Brandon Sanderson, I received two very distinct impressions of the books. On the whole, Skyward was a very good book. It begins as a tale of learning and personal betterment, morphing into a tale of a broken setting, discovery, and achieving against the odds. The end of the book leaves huge questions and massive potential for a sequel. When the sequel did come out, I gave it a try. I did finish it, but not as enthusiastically as the first. For Starsight, I found that the first thirty-one chapters were written for a younger audience, relying on just-in-time moments, tropes, and deus-ex-machina. Chapter thiry-two and after, however, seemes to pick up the original intent and meaning of book one.

  • Reflection 108

    May 16, 2018 - CS108 blog

    From our 108 course in general, I learned a great deal - so much so, it would be more productive to re-upload the study guide. The presentations throughout the last week, however, help pinpoint these. For example, student J.K.’s presentation on the battle royale genre demonstrated the creation of something new in the history of digital games. It also demonstrated the gathering of old mechanics to create new dynamics. In student A. R.’s board game, I was able to see the power of iterative design. I played both the alpha and the finished game, which had gone from 100% chance based to implementing strategies resulting from decisions. If I could have learned one other thing, it would have been more in-depth the aspects of card games. (If I could choose another final project, it would have been this.) This is because board games, our starting point, feel unique (both ludologically and artistically) between each other, while card games feel more universal (at least with standardized decks).

  • Bridgebound Alpha

    May 2, 2018 - CS108 blog

    For the final project of our game studies class, my team has put forward the idea of Bridgebound. (It is a name I thought of, but our lead has humorously, occasionally referred to it as Bridgeborne, for his love of another game series). This is a 3D, third-person game developed by our team of four in the Unreal Engine. It is single-player, designed to be a sole, engrossing boss fight, which relies on unique mechanics and gives a full-fledged experience. (We have included menus, lore, the main game loop, custom assets, win/loss conditions, and more).

  • An Ingress into Ingress

    Apr 25, 2018 - CS108 blog

    I had first heard about the mobile video game Ingress years ago, when a close relative of mine tried it out at a college campus. Back then, I had never put much thought into it, because I had heard it ate too much battery life, and I hadn’t given alternate reality games much credit. Experiencing it today as a team, in class, let me see how this game could draw and maintain an audience. (However, it still cannot maintain my battery). I came with the game prepared, having selected my team according to the class’s instructions. We were divided across the sides of the class by team, and then let loose. A good friend of mine couldn’t get the app working (a strange zoom bug), so he joined me. In the process, we acquired a third person on the same team who had never played before. We poked at the interface and awed at the jargon, having no idea how to do anything. Thus, we decided our best course of action was to walk to the gray portals.
