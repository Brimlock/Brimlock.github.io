One Tough Program

Dec 8, 2019

One Tough Puzzle is the name of a classic jigsaw puzzle that I received five to ten years ago. I had heard of a similar puzzle without a pattern, and reckoning the difficulty, I had never actually bothered. But we live in the information age, and deciding to brush up on my C skills, I wrote a program to solve it. This could be genericized to solve any jigsaw puzzle (if the puzzle’s joints are enumerable).

The Puzzle

The puzzle only includes nine pieces; however they are all blank. This would be 9! permutations (possible solutions) to number 362,880. This is as advertised, over 300,000. That does not account for all rotations of the pieces as theyare placed. On top of that, every configuration is isometric to three others (because the solution itself could be rotated. This ultimately calculates to 9!*4**9/4 or 9!*4**8 total possible solutions (23,781,703,680). This should hypothetically… not be worth solving.

The Program

I began drafting out some simple definitions to begin solving the puzzle. These were typedef ed and the slot for clubs was chosen to be 0 (instead of -4) so that the enumeration would fit into three bits.

The next phase was to right out the general rules to the program. This was mostly restricted to the data structures and their representation of the puzzle within the program.

Finally, in the third stage, I defined the algorithm. This scrap of paper hardly can account for a design doc, but it served. Maybe for the next projects, I will have to step up my game… :D Anyway, after these were defined, I ended up taking a long hiatus from the program. Between the time, I even ended up working on the Genetic Cellular Automaton in some between time. Then, many months later, vacation hit and I returned to the grind.

The Return

During a Thanksgiving movie with family, I drafted a more in-depth pseudocode to the program. I know… I’m a family man. It actually worked out though! I got to watch A White Christmas, relaxing, and doing what I love – coding – at the same time. Of course, my algorithing bled into the next movie. My attention to it bled as well, but the resounding disappointment by my family to the movie let me know… yep, didn’t miss much.

Unfortunately, when I began again on my C files, the road was bumpy. Or buggy. I squished many, but the program refused to work. It turned into a bit of an obsession… and during the tail end of the vacation, I probably paid less attention to the present than I should have… “Don’t you get tired of programming?” One of my family asked. Yep! She even solved the puzzle in about twenty minutes. That was a bit funny to me. I was impressed with her achievement, and they all attested it to the “human element.” I think, however, the puzzle is a bit simpler than at first glance. One can tell that some types of joints will need to be at the edges (the excess joints), effectively eliminating manifold permutations. I acted flabbergasted for fun (for somewhy), but really solving the puzzle had long not been the objective. To me, these side projects aren’t work – the project itself is the exciting challenge.

Two weeks later, on December 7th, I squished the last bug. I printed the solution. Thankfully, it worked.
